### Sourcing Transport partners: Local Chinese Forwarders vs. International logistics lines

When sourcing transport partners, importers often face a choice between engaging a local Chinese freight forwarder or working with the local branch of a larger international logistics company. Both have their merits and drawbacks. Local Chinese forwarders, often referred to as "庄家货代" (Zhuangjia Huodai), typically offer competitive rates due to their deep local networks and direct relationships with carriers. However, their communication might be less polished for Western buyers, and their operational transparency can sometimes be a concern without proper vetting. In contrast, international logistics lines provide robust global networks, standardized services, and often better English-speaking support, but their rates can be higher. We at GoCNScout recommend evaluating both options through our database, filtering by their registered capital, export volume, and NVOCC status to make an informed decision.

### The Risk of letting the factory handle logistics: Unchecked destination port fees

A common pitfall we've observed in our sourcing experience is allowing the factory to arrange shipping on a CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight) or CFR (Cost and Freight) basis. While seemingly convenient, this often leads to exorbitant and opaque destination port charges. Factories typically choose the cheapest freight forwarder on their end, who then partners with an unknown agent at the destination port. This agent, having no direct relationship with you, can levy excessive fees for handling, warehousing, and customs clearance, effectively holding your cargo hostage until paid. We strongly advise importers to control their logistics chain by nominating their own trusted freight forwarder, preferably on an FOB (Free On Board) or EXW (Ex Works) basis, to maintain transparency and cost control. This proactive approach prevents the "surprise bill" scenario we've seen many clients face in 2026.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a customs bond and why do I need one for US imports?

A customs bond is a contract legally required by U.S. Customs and Border Protection ([CBP.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/basic-import-export/customs-bonds)) for most commercial imports valued over $2,500, or for goods subject to other federal agency requirements. It guarantees that all duties, taxes, and fees owed to the U.S. government will be paid. Without a customs bond, your goods cannot clear customs and will be held at the port, incurring significant demurrage and detention charges. It acts as a financial guarantee, protecting the government in case the importer fails to meet their financial obligations. Importers can purchase either a single-entry bond for a specific shipment or a continuous bond for multiple shipments over a 12-month period, which is more cost-effective for frequent importers in 2026.

### How to calculate chargeability weight for air freight consolidation?

For air freight, carriers charge based on the "chargeable weight," which is the greater of the actual gross weight (in kilograms) or the volumetric weight. Volumetric weight is calculated using a specific formula: (Length x Width x Height in centimeters) / 6000. For example, if a carton measures 100cm x 80cm x 60cm, its volumetric weight would be (100 * 80 * 60) / 6000 = 80 kg. If the actual weight of this carton is only 50 kg, the carrier will charge based on 80 kg. If the actual weight is 90 kg, they will charge based on 90 kg. When consolidating multiple shipments, your freight forwarder will calculate the total chargeable weight for the entire consignment, optimizing space and cost where possible. Always clarify this calculation with your forwarder to avoid surprises.
