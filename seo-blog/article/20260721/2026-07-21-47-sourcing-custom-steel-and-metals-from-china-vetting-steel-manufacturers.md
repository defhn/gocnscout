### Vetting Metal Grades: 304 vs. 316 Stainless Steel standards compliance

Understanding and verifying specific metal grades is paramount. For instance, the choice between 304 and 316 stainless steel isn't merely about cost; it's about performance in specific environments. While 304 stainless steel offers good corrosion resistance and is widely used, 316 stainless steel contains molybdenum, significantly enhancing its resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion in chloride-rich environments, such as marine applications or chemical processing. We consistently advise our clients to explicitly state the required grade and demand verifiable proof of compliance. Without stringent checks, a supplier might substitute a cheaper, less resistant grade, leading to premature product failure. International standards like those from the [International Organization for Standardization (ISO)](https://www.iso.org/home.html) provide benchmarks, but the onus is on the buyer to ensure these are met.

### The Risk of Material Substitution: Demanding Mill Test Certificates (MTC)

One of the most insidious risks when **sourcing custom steel from China** is material substitution. We've encountered cases where suppliers, under pressure to cut costs or meet tight deadlines, have shipped lower-grade materials than specified. The only reliable defense against this is the Mill Test Certificate (MTC). An MTC is a document issued by the steel mill, certifying the chemical and mechanical properties of a specific batch of steel. It's essentially the birth certificate of your material. However, not all MTCs are genuine. In 2026, sophisticated counterfeit MTCs are a growing concern. We guide our clients to demand MTCs directly from the mill, not just the trading company, and to cross-reference batch numbers and chemical compositions against independent lab tests if any doubt arises. This proactive approach is critical to mitigating financial and reputational risks.

### What are the differences between hot rolled and cold rolled steel options?

Hot rolled steel is processed at high temperatures (over 1,000°F), making it easier to form and resulting in a rougher surface finish with slight dimensional variations. It's typically used for structural components, frames, and parts where precise dimensions aren't critical. Cold rolled steel, on the other hand, is processed at or near room temperature after hot rolling, leading to a smoother surface, tighter dimensional tolerances, and increased strength. It's often preferred for applications requiring aesthetic appeal, precision, and superior surface quality, such as automotive body panels or appliances. Your choice depends entirely on the specific application, required finish, and dimensional accuracy.
