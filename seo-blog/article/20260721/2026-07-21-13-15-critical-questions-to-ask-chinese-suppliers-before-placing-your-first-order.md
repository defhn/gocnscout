## Screening Suppliers: Essential Questions to Ask Chinese Suppliers

![A checklist of essential questions to ask Chinese suppliers for vetting in 2026](/images/15-critical-questions-to-ask-chinese-suppliers-before-placing-your-first-order-body-1.png)

### Why Standard RFQ Inquiries Fail to Filter Out Middlemen

Many importers start their sourcing journey with generic RFQs that focus solely on price, quantity, and delivery time. While these are important, they are insufficient for identifying genuine manufacturers versus trading companies or even outright scammers. Our data engineering team at GoCNScout has analyzed millions of trade records and found that suppliers often present themselves as factories, even when they are not. A standard RFQ, asking "Are you a factory?" will almost always yield a "Yes," regardless of the truth. This superficial approach leads to inflated costs, lack of direct control over production, and increased risk of miscommunication or quality issues down the line.

For instance, in early 2026, one of our clients, an Amazon FBA seller, received a competitive quote for custom packaging. They proceeded with a small order based on a standard RFQ. Only after a critical defect rate emerged and direct factory communication became impossible did they realize they were dealing with a trading company that had minimal influence over the actual production line. This experience underscores the need for a more robust questioning strategy that probes deeper into their operational structure and capabilities.

### The Checklist: 15 Questions on Capacity, Tooling, and Raw Materials

To effectively vet a potential partner, we recommend asking these 15 critical **questions to ask Chinese suppliers**. These questions are designed to uncover vital information about their manufacturing capabilities, quality control processes, and overall reliability. Remember, a reputable factory will be transparent and willing to provide detailed answers.

  - What is your monthly production capacity for [specific product]?

  - Can you provide a list of your primary manufacturing equipment and their ages?

  - Do you own the tooling/molds for [specific component], or are they outsourced?

  - What are your typical lead times for sample production and mass production?

  - How do you manage your raw material supply chain? Do you have multiple approved suppliers?

  - What quality control checks are performed at each stage of production (incoming, in-process, final)?

  - Can you provide copies of your ISO 9001 or other relevant quality certifications?

  - What is your defect rate target, and how do you handle non-conforming products?

  - Do you have experience exporting to [your country/region]? Can you provide references?

  - What are your standard payment terms (e.g., 30% T/T upfront, 70% upon shipment)?

  - Which Incoterms do you typically work with (e.g., FOB, EXW, DDP)?

  - How do you handle intellectual property protection for custom designs?

  - What is your policy on factory audits by third-party inspection companies?

  - Can you provide a copy of your business license and export license?

  - What is your minimum order quantity (MOQ) for this product, and is it negotiable?

These questions, when answered thoroughly, provide a comprehensive picture of a supplier's suitability. For deeper insights, consider using our [industry analysis reports](/reports) which often include common pitfalls and best practices for specific product categories.

## Advanced Practices on How to Communicate with Chinese Factories

![Effective communication strategies for B2B buyers with Chinese factories in 2026](/images/15-critical-questions-to-ask-chinese-suppliers-before-placing-your-first-order-body-2.png)

### Avoiding Misunderstandings Due to Language Barriers and Wechat Translation

Effective communication is paramount when learning **how to communicate with Chinese factories**. While many Chinese sales representatives have good English skills, nuances can still be lost, especially when relying on automated translation tools like WeChat's built-in translator. Technical specifications, quality standards, and contractual terms require absolute clarity. We've observed situations where a simple misunderstanding of a measurement unit or a quality tolerance led to an entire batch of products being rejected. To mitigate this, we recommend:

  - **Use Visual Aids**: Provide detailed drawings, CAD files, and reference photos with annotations.

  - **Confirm Understanding**: Ask the supplier to rephrase or summarize key points in their own words.

  - **Formalize Critical Information**: Always follow up verbal or chat conversations with an email summarizing agreements.

  - **Be Specific**: Avoid vague language. Instead of "good quality," specify "AQL 2.5 for critical defects."

  - **Consider a Translator**: For complex negotiations or technical discussions, a professional translator can be invaluable.

Remember, patience and persistence are key. Building a strong relationship often means investing time in clear, unambiguous communication from the outset.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I ask for lower MOQs without sounding unprofessional?

To request lower Minimum Order Quantities (MOQs) professionally, frame your request by explaining your market entry strategy or testing phase. Instead of simply asking "Can you do less?", try: "We are launching this product in a new market segment in 2026 and would like to start with a trial order of [desired quantity] to assess market demand. If successful, we anticipate scaling up to your standard MOQ of [supplier's MOQ] within X months." This shows commitment and a clear path to larger orders, making the supplier more amenable to negotiation. Be prepared to potentially pay a slightly higher unit price for smaller quantities.

### Should I communicate with the general manager or the sales rep?

Initially, it is generally best to communicate with the sales representative. They are your primary point of contact, trained to handle inquiries, and often have direct access to internal departments. Escalating directly to the general manager too early can be seen as bypassing established channels and may not be well-received. However, if you encounter persistent communication issues, significant delays, or critical unresolved problems, politely requesting to speak with a senior manager or the general manager is appropriate. Always maintain a respectful and professional tone, explaining the situation clearly and concisely.
