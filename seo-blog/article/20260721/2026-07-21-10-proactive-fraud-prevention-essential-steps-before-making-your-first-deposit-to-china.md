Before you commit your first dollar, we urge you to implement these five critical verification steps:

- **Verify Business Registration & Export Licenses**: Cross-reference the supplier's business license against official Chinese government databases and confirm their export capabilities.

- **Conduct Comprehensive Bank Account Scrutiny**: Ensure the beneficiary bank account is a corporate account matching the registered company name, not a personal account or one from a different entity.

- **Demand Live Video Factory Audits**: Insist on a real-time video tour of the production facility, verifying physical presence and operational capacity.

## Procurement Risk Safeguards: How to Avoid China Sourcing Scams

![Illustration of a digital lock and key, representing secure procurement and risk management](/images/proactive-fraud-prevention-essential-steps-before-making-your-first-deposit-to-china-body-1.png)

The global trade landscape, while offering immense opportunities, is also rife with potential pitfalls. For importers, the journey from initial contact to final delivery is a minefield of regulatory hurdles, quality control challenges, and, most critically, fraud risks. We at GoCNScout have observed that many sourcing scams specifically target the initial deposit phase, exploiting the buyer's eagerness to secure a deal and kickstart production.

### The Critical First Deposit: Protecting Your Initial Cash Outflow Safety

The first deposit, typically 30% of the total order value, is often the most vulnerable point in the entire sourcing process. This upfront payment is intended to cover material costs and initiate production. However, it also represents the scammer's primary target. Once this capital leaves your account, recovering it can be an arduous, often impossible, task. Our data engineering team has analyzed countless cases where importers lost significant sums due to inadequate due diligence at this crucial juncture. It's not just about losing money; it's about losing time, market share, and trust in the global supply chain.

### Sourcing Scammers' Target Scenarios and Setup Dynamics

Sourcing scammers are increasingly sophisticated, adapting their tactics to exploit common importer pain points. They often target buyers who are new to direct sourcing, those under tight deadlines, or those seeking unusually low prices. Common scenarios include:

- **The "Too Good to Be True" Offer**: Unrealistic pricing designed to lure buyers away from legitimate suppliers.

- **Impersonation Scams**: Posing as reputable factories or well-known trading companies, often by creating fake websites or email domains.

- **"Gold Supplier" Fallacy**: Relying solely on platform badges without independent verification, as these can sometimes be manipulated or represent outdated information.

- **Bank Account Change Scams**: Intercepting communications to request payment to a fraudulent account, often claiming "new banking details" or "urgent changes."

These setups are designed to create a sense of urgency and legitimacy, pushing buyers to make quick decisions without thorough verification. Understanding these dynamics is the first step in building a robust defense.

## Spotting Essential Red Flags When Sourcing from China Prior to Payment

![Illustration of a magnifying glass highlighting a red flag, symbolizing careful scrutiny in sourcing](/images/proactive-fraud-prevention-essential-steps-before-making-your-first-deposit-to-china-body-2.png)

Before any financial transaction, recognizing the subtle (and sometimes not-so-subtle) warning signs is paramount. Our experience at GoCNScout has taught us that these "red flags when sourcing from China" are often present, but easily overlooked by an eager buyer. Developing an acute awareness of these indicators can save you from significant financial loss and operational disruption.

### Rushing the Purchase Order Without Standard Material Vetting

A legitimate supplier will understand and respect the need for thorough due diligence, especially concerning material specifications and quality. A significant red flag emerges when a supplier pressures you to finalize a Purchase Order (PO) and make a deposit without allowing sufficient time for comprehensive material vetting, sample testing, or detailed specification reviews. This haste often indicates an attempt to bypass critical inspection points where their fraudulent intentions or substandard capabilities might be exposed. For instance, if a supplier insists on a PO before you've received and approved a pre-production sample that meets your exact technical drawings and quality standards (e.g., ISO 9001 compliance for manufacturing processes), proceed with extreme caution. We recommend adhering to a strict [SOP for sample approval](/reports), regardless of supplier pressure.

### Reluctance to Share Live Video Audits or Factory Coordinates

In 2026, with widespread access to video conferencing tools, there is no legitimate reason for a supplier to refuse a live video tour of their factory. A genuine manufacturer will be proud to showcase their facilities, production lines, and quality control processes. Reluctance or outright refusal to conduct a live video audit, or to provide precise factory coordinates (address, GPS location) that can be cross-referenced with satellite imagery or local business directories, is a major warning sign. Scammers often operate from small offices, trading companies, or even residential addresses, lacking actual production capabilities. They might offer pre-recorded videos or generic photos, but these can be easily faked. Insist on a real-time, interactive video call where you can ask to see specific areas of the factory, machinery, or even staff working on similar products.

> Pro Tip: During a live video audit, ask the supplier to show you a specific, unique item or a current newspaper to verify the date and location. This simple trick can expose pre-recorded video scams.

## Execution Framework: 5 Proactive Checks Before Wiring Any Deposit

![Illustration of a checklist and magnifying glass, symbolizing a meticulous execution framework for verification](/images/proactive-fraud-prevention-essential-steps-before-making-your-first-deposit-to-china-body-3.png)

At GoCNScout, we advocate for a robust, multi-layered verification process. This framework is designed to empower importers with actionable steps to confirm supplier legitimacy and mitigate fraud risks before any financial commitment. Our approach combines traditional due diligence with cutting-edge trade data analysis to provide unparalleled transparency.

### Executing Sample Vetting and Checking Corporate Bank Accounts

Before wiring any deposit, two critical checks must be completed. Firstly, rigorous sample vetting is non-negotiable. This involves not just receiving a sample, but subjecting it to the same quality control tests and performance benchmarks you would apply to your final product. Check dimensions, materials, functionality, and compliance with relevant certifications (e.g., CE for Europe, FCC for the US). Document any discrepancies and ensure they are addressed before moving forward.

Secondly, scrutinize the corporate bank account details. The beneficiary bank account must belong to the exact company name registered on the business license and contract. Never transfer funds to a personal account, an account in a different company name, or an account located in a third country without explicit, independently verified justification. Request a copy of their bank account information on official company letterhead. Cross-reference the bank's location and reputation. Fraudsters frequently attempt to divert funds to personal accounts or accounts in tax havens. Always verify these details with a phone call to a known contact at the supplier, using a number you've independently verified, not one provided in an email requesting a change.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the safest deposit percentage for custom tooling?

For custom tooling, the safest deposit percentage is typically around 30-50%. However, this can vary based on the complexity and cost of the tooling. It's crucial to negotiate a payment schedule that ties subsequent payments to specific milestones, such as tooling completion, sample approval, and mass production commencement. Always ensure your contract (NNN agreement) clearly outlines these stages and the conditions for each payment release. For high-value tooling, consider using an escrow service or a Letter of Credit (LC) to add an extra layer of security, though these options come with additional costs and complexities.

### How to handle payments during Chinese holidays?

Handling payments during Chinese holidays requires careful planning to avoid delays and potential scam attempts. Major holidays like Chinese New Year (Spring Festival) and Golden Week can halt banking operations for extended periods. Always schedule payments well in advance of these holidays, allowing extra buffer time for processing. Be wary of urgent payment requests just before or during holidays, as scammers often exploit these periods when communication lines might be slower. Confirm all payment instructions with your supplier *before* the holiday period begins, and avoid making any transfers during the actual holiday week unless absolutely necessary and thoroughly verified.

### What should I do if a supplier changes bank details?

If a supplier suddenly requests a change in bank details, treat it as an immediate and severe red flag. This is a common tactic used in business email compromise (BEC) scams. **Do not proceed with any payment based on an email request alone.** Immediately contact your known supplier representative via a previously established, independently verified communication channel (e.g., a phone number you've used before, not one provided in the suspicious email). Verify the change verbally and ask for official documentation on company letterhead. If possible, have your bank verify the new account details. If you cannot independently verify the change, assume it's a scam and do not transfer funds. Report any suspicious activity to your bank and relevant authorities like the [FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)](https://www.ic3.gov/) in the US.
