### Standard Sourcing Payments: Wire Transfer, L/C, and Escrow Overview

When dealing with Chinese suppliers, three primary payment methods dominate the B2B landscape: Telegraphic Transfer (T/T), Letter of Credit (L/C), and Escrow services. Each offers distinct advantages and carries specific risks that importers must weigh. T/T, or bank wire transfer, is the most common due to its speed and relatively low cost. However, it offers minimal buyer protection once funds are sent. Letters of Credit, while more complex and costly, provide a high level of security by involving banks as intermediaries, guaranteeing payment only upon fulfillment of specified conditions. Escrow services, like those offered by third-party platforms, hold funds until both parties confirm satisfactory completion of the transaction, offering a balanced approach to risk management.

### The Safe Rule of 30/70 T/T Terms: Reducing Financial Exposure

For most B2B transactions with established Chinese suppliers, the 30% upfront and 70% balance upon shipment (or against B/L copy) T/T term is considered a standard and relatively safe practice. This structure ensures the supplier has initial capital to begin production, while the majority of your payment is contingent on the goods being ready for dispatch. We've seen countless cases where deviations from this standard, such as requests for 50% or more upfront, signal a higher risk. In 2026, any supplier insisting on unusually high upfront payments without a clear, justifiable reason should raise a red flag. This payment structure aligns the supplier's incentive with timely production and quality control, as their final payment is tied to successful delivery. For new suppliers or high-value orders, consider even more conservative splits like 20/80 or explore L/C options.

### Aligning Beneficiary Accounts with Corporate Tax Registrations

One of the most crucial verification steps is ensuring that the beneficiary bank account name precisely matches the supplier's officially registered company name on their business license and tax registration documents. Any deviation—even a minor spelling difference or the use of a personal name—is a significant warning sign. In 2026, Chinese regulations are stringent about corporate banking. A legitimate factory or trading company will always have a corporate bank account under its registered legal name. We advise requesting a copy of their business license and comparing it directly with the bank details provided for payment. If a supplier claims their corporate account is "under maintenance" or "for internal reasons" they need a personal account, it's almost certainly a scam. You can use the GoCNScout [supplier database](/database) to cross-reference registered company names with their export records, adding an extra layer of verification.

### How Sourcing Scammers Redirect Wires to Fraudulent Bank Branches

We've observed a recurring pattern in sourcing fraud where scammers intercept communications or impersonate legitimate suppliers to redirect payments. In a notable case from early 2026, an importer was working with a verified Chinese manufacturer for custom electronics. Days before the final 70% payment was due, the importer received an email, seemingly from the supplier, stating their bank account had changed due to an "internal restructuring." The new account details were for a different bank, and crucially, a different beneficiary name. Fortunately, the importer used GoCNScout's [industry intelligence reports](/reports) and noticed the new bank was not typically used by established manufacturers in that sector. A quick call to the original supplier (using a verified phone number, not the one from the suspicious email) confirmed the fraud. The scammer had created a fake email address almost identical to the supplier's and timed the attack perfectly. This highlights the importance of verifying *any* change in payment instructions through multiple, independently verified channels, especially phone calls, and always cross-referencing with official company records.

> Pro Tip / Warning: Always verify any change in payment instructions via a phone call to a pre-verified contact number, not just email. Scammers are adept at email spoofing.

## Best Practices for Setting Up International Escrow and Letters of Credit

![Illustration of secure international payment processes](/images/safe-payment-methods-for-chinese-suppliers-tt-l-c-escrow-and-how-to-use-them-body-3.png)

For high-value transactions or when working with new suppliers, T/T might not offer sufficient protection. This is where more secure instruments like Letters of Credit (L/C) and international escrow services become invaluable. While they involve more administrative overhead and potentially higher costs, the enhanced security they provide can be well worth the investment, especially when dealing with significant financial exposure in 2026.

### Working with Chinese State Banks to Open Secure L/C

A Letter of Credit (L/C) is a financial instrument issued by a bank, guaranteeing payment to the seller (exporter) on behalf of the buyer (importer), provided that the seller presents specified documents that comply with the L/C terms. For transactions with Chinese suppliers, working with reputable Chinese state-owned banks such as Bank of China, ICBC, or China Construction Bank can add an extra layer of security. These banks are well-versed in international trade finance and adhere strictly to Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits (UCP 600) rules, issued by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC). When setting up an L/C, ensure all conditions are meticulously detailed, including quality inspections, shipping documents (Bill of Lading), and delivery deadlines. This shifts the risk from the buyer directly to the banks, contingent on document compliance. For more information on international trade regulations, refer to official sources like the [World Trade Organization (WTO)](https://www.wto.org/).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should I do if a supplier asks for a personal bank wire?

You should immediately cease communication and consider this a major red flag. Legitimate Chinese companies operate with corporate bank accounts. Requests for personal bank wires, especially to accounts outside of China or in a name different from the registered company, are almost always indicative of fraud. Do not proceed with payment and report the incident if you suspect a scam. Always verify the beneficiary name against the official business registration documents.

### Is Trade Assurance on Alibaba 100% safe?

Alibaba Trade Assurance offers a layer of protection, but it is not 100% foolproof. It provides safeguards for payment and product quality, but disputes can still arise, and the resolution process can be lengthy. It's best viewed as an enhanced escrow service rather than an absolute guarantee. We recommend combining Trade Assurance with your own independent supplier verification, quality control inspections, and clear, detailed contracts to maximize your security in 2026.

### What are the bank transfer processing fees for China wires?

Bank transfer processing fees for wires to China vary significantly depending on your bank, the amount being transferred, and any intermediary banks involved. Typically, sender fees can range from $25 to $50 USD per transaction. The recipient's bank in China may also levy a small receiving fee. It's crucial to clarify with your bank and the supplier who bears which fees (e.g., "all bank charges on sender" or "shared charges") to avoid unexpected deductions from your payment. For large volumes, consider specialized cross-border payment partners that offer more competitive rates.
