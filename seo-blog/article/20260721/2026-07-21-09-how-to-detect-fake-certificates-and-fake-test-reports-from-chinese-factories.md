### The Risk of Fake CE, RoHS, and FCC Testing Certificates

Product safety and compliance certificates like CE (for European markets), RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances), and FCC (Federal Communications Commission for electronics in the US) are not mere formalities; they are legal declarations of conformity. A fake CE certificate, for instance, means your product has not undergone the necessary safety assessments, potentially endangering end-users. In 2026, regulatory bodies are more vigilant than ever, employing advanced methods to detect fraudulent documentation. We've encountered scenarios where factories would simply edit dates or company names on old, unrelated reports, presenting them as valid for new products. This deceptive practice is a ticking time bomb for importers.

### Searching Lab Databases (SGS, TÜV, Intertek) to Validate Test Reports

The most reliable way to validate a test report is to go directly to the source: the testing laboratory. Reputable labs like SGS, TÜV Rheinland, Intertek, and Bureau Veritas maintain online databases where you can input certificate numbers or QR codes to verify their authenticity. We advise clients to always request the full test report, not just the summary certificate, and then use the unique identifier on the report to search the lab's official portal. In one instance, a client was presented with an SGS report for a batch of toys. A quick search on the SGS database revealed the certificate number belonged to a completely different product category from a different manufacturer, saving our client from a potentially disastrous import.

### Matching Certificate Holders with Actual Factory Registries

A common tactic used by fraudulent suppliers is to present certificates issued to a different company, often a larger, reputable factory, or even a trading company that has no direct manufacturing capability. When you receive a certificate, meticulously compare the company name and address listed on the certificate with the business registration details of the factory you are dealing with. These details should also align with the information on their business license. Discrepancies are major red flags. Our **China supplier background check** services at GoCNScout include cross-referencing these details against official Chinese corporate registration databases, ensuring that the entity holding the certificate is indeed the entity you intend to do business with.

## Frequently Asked Questions

Navigating the intricacies of supplier verification often leads to specific questions about certifications and compliance. Here, we address some common queries that arise when you **verify Chinese manufacturer online**.

### Can a trading company hold product certificates?

Yes, a trading company can legally hold product certificates, especially if they are the "legal manufacturer" or "importer of record" responsible for placing the product on the market. However, this doesn't mean they manufactured the product themselves. The critical point is to understand the relationship between the trading company, the actual factory, and the certificate. We advise importers to always seek transparency regarding the actual production facility, even when dealing with a trading company that holds the certificates. This ensures you understand the full supply chain and can conduct factory audits if necessary.

### How do I verify a CE Declaration of Conformity?

Verifying a CE Declaration of Conformity (DoC) involves several steps. First, ensure the DoC is signed by a responsible person within the manufacturer or their authorized representative in the EU. Second, check that it clearly lists the product, relevant directives (e.g., Low Voltage Directive, EMC Directive), and harmonized standards applied. Third, if a Notified Body was involved (for certain high-risk products), verify their identification number and check their accreditation status on the NANDO database (New Approach Notified and Designated Organisations). Finally, cross-reference the manufacturer's details on the DoC with their official business registration.

### What are the risks of using the supplier's test report?

The primary risk of solely relying on a supplier's test report is that it might be fake, outdated, or manipulated. Suppliers might present reports from different products, different factories, or even entirely fabricated documents. Without independent verification, you are vulnerable to importing non-compliant goods, leading to customs issues, product recalls, and legal liabilities.
