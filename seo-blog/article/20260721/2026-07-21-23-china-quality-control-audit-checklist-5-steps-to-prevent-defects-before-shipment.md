### Incoming Quality Control (IQC) vs. In-Process Inspections (IPQC)

Understanding the different stages of quality control is crucial for any effective **China quality control audit checklist**.

  - **Incoming Quality Control (IQC)**: This involves inspecting raw materials and components as they arrive at the factory. It's the first line of defense, ensuring that the building blocks of your product meet specifications before production even begins. Failing to implement IQC can lead to an entire batch of products being compromised by substandard materials.

  - **In-Process Quality Control (IPQC)**: Also known as During Production Inspection (DPI), IPQC involves checks at various stages of the manufacturing process. This allows for early detection of defects, preventing the accumulation of faulty products and enabling timely corrective actions. For instance, checking solder joints on circuit boards during assembly, rather than after the final product is sealed, saves immense rework time and cost.

Both IQC and IPQC are vital components of a comprehensive quality assurance strategy, far more effective than relying solely on a Pre-Shipment Inspection (PSI).

### Setting up Acceptable Quality Limits (AQL 2.5) for Inspections

Before any inspection, you must define your Acceptable Quality Limits (AQL). AQL is a statistical sampling method widely used in quality control to determine if a batch of products meets specified quality standards. For most consumer goods, an AQL of 2.5 for major defects and 4.0 for minor defects is a common industry standard. This means that for a given sample size, if the number of defects exceeds the pre-defined limit, the entire batch can be rejected. Clearly communicating your AQL standards to your Chinese supplier and the inspection agency is a critical step in setting expectations and ensuring accountability. Without a defined AQL, "quality" becomes subjective, leading to disputes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

![Two procurement managers discussing pre-production vs. during-production audits in a factory setting in 2026.](/images/china-quality-control-audit-checklist-5-steps-to-prevent-defects-before-shipment-body-3.png)

We often receive questions from procurement managers and FBA sellers about the nuances of quality control in China. Here are some common inquiries related to the **China quality control audit checklist**.

### What is the difference between pre-production and during-production audits?

Pre-production audits, also known as Initial Production Checks (IPC), occur before mass production begins. Their primary goal is to verify that the factory has all necessary raw materials, components, machinery, and production plans in place, and that the initial samples meet specifications. This audit ensures that the factory is ready to start production correctly. During-production audits (DPI or IPQC), on the other hand, take place when 20-80% of the goods have been produced. These audits monitor the manufacturing process, identify defects early, and ensure that the production schedule and quality standards are being maintained. Both are critical steps in a comprehensive **China quality control audit checklist** to prevent defects.

### Who pays for re-inspection if the QC audit fails?

Typically, the responsibility for re-inspection fees depends on the terms negotiated in your Purchase Order (PO) or supply agreement. In most cases, if the initial QC audit fails due to the supplier's fault (e.g., manufacturing defects, incorrect specifications), the supplier is expected to cover the cost of the re-inspection. However, if the failure is due to unclear specifications from the buyer or changes requested after production began, the buyer might bear the cost. It is crucial to clearly define these terms, including Incoterms like FOB or DDP, in your contract before production starts to avoid disputes in 2026. A well-defined **China quality control audit checklist** and contract are your best defense.
