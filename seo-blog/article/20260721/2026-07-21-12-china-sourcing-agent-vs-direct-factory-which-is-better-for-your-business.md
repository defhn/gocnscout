Navigating the complexities of B2B sourcing in China can be daunting. For many global procurement managers, Amazon FBA sellers, and importers, the fundamental question remains: should you work with a China sourcing agent or go directly to the factory? This decision significantly impacts your costs, quality control, and overall supply chain resilience. At GoCNScout, we've seen firsthand the pitfalls and triumphs associated with both approaches.

Before you commit to any supplier or agent, here’s an actionable checklist of 5 critical things to verify in 2026:

  - **Verify Business Registration**: Confirm the supplier's legal entity and registration status in China via official government databases.

  - **Check Export History**: Analyze their actual export records to ensure they are a legitimate exporter, not just a trading company posing as a factory.

  - **Assess Production Capacity**: Request factory audit reports (e.g., ISO 9001, BSCI, Sedex) or conduct virtual tours to gauge their real manufacturing capabilities.

  - **Review Quality Control Protocols**: Understand their internal QC processes and demand pre-shipment inspection (PSI) reports from independent third parties.

  - **Clarify Payment Terms & Incoterms**: Ensure all payment milestones and Incoterms (e.g., FOB, DDP, EXW) are clearly defined in a legally binding contract to avoid disputes.

## Sourcing Channel Strategy: China Sourcing Agent vs Direct Factory

![Comparison of China Sourcing Agent vs. Direct Factory benefits and drawbacks in 2026](/images/china-sourcing-agent-vs-direct-factory-which-is-better-for-your-business-body-1.png)

The choice between a China sourcing agent and a direct factory isn't merely about cost; it's about control, risk management, and strategic alignment with your business goals. While agents often promise convenience, their opaque pricing structures and potential for misaligned incentives can introduce significant challenges. Conversely, direct factory engagement, while offering transparency, demands a higher level of internal expertise and resource allocation.

### Understanding Sourcing Intermediaries: Markup Brokers vs. Fee-based Sourcing Agents

In our experience, many "sourcing agents" are, in reality, trading companies or brokers who add a significant markup to factory prices without full disclosure. This isn't inherently bad if they provide genuine value, but it becomes problematic when they masquerade as direct factory representatives or charge exorbitant, hidden fees. A legitimate, fee-based sourcing agent, on the other hand, operates on a transparent commission (typically 3-10% of the order value) and acts as your eyes and ears on the ground, managing quality control, logistics, and communication. They can be invaluable for complex projects or when you lack local presence.

However, the core limitation of traditional sourcing agents and brokers lies in their inherent incentive to maintain information asymmetry. They thrive on being the sole conduit, often withholding direct factory contact information to protect their margins. This creates a dependency that can be costly and difficult to unwind, especially as your business scales.

### When Startups Should Avoid Direct Factory Deals Due to MOQ Constraints

For startups or businesses with low order volumes, directly engaging factories can be challenging due to Minimum Order Quantity (MOQ) constraints. Factories, especially large-scale manufacturers, prioritize efficiency and large production runs. If your MOQ is significantly below their standard, they might:

  - Refuse your order outright.

  - Quote a much higher per-unit price to compensate for setup costs.

  - Assign your order to a less experienced production line, potentially impacting quality.

In such scenarios, a sourcing agent might consolidate orders from multiple small buyers to meet a factory's MOQ, or connect you with smaller, more flexible factories. However, this convenience comes at a price, often in the form of higher unit costs and less direct control over the manufacturing process. It's a trade-off that requires careful consideration of your budget, volume, and risk tolerance for 2026.

### What is the typical commission fee for a Chinese sourcing agent?

The typical commission fee for a Chinese sourcing agent can vary widely, but it generally ranges from **3% to 10%** of the total order value. This percentage often depends on the order size, product complexity, and the scope of services provided (e.g., quality control, logistics, factory audits). Some agents may also charge a flat fee or a combination of both. It's crucial to clarify all fees upfront and ensure they are transparently itemized in your contract to avoid hidden costs.
