## Verifying Authenticity: Check If a Company in China is Legit

When sourcing from China, the initial step of verifying a supplier's authenticity is often the most overlooked, yet it's the bedrock of a secure trade relationship. Many buyers focus solely on product samples or pricing, neglecting the crucial due diligence required to **check if a company in China is legit**. This oversight can lead to significant financial and operational setbacks.

![Section 1 illustration](/images/is-your-chinese-supplier-legitimate-how-to-match-corporate-names-bank-details-body-1.png)

### The Risk of Dealing with Shell Companies and Brokers

A common pitfall for international buyers is unknowingly dealing with shell companies or trading brokers masquerading as direct manufacturers. These entities often present themselves with impressive websites and product catalogs, but lack the actual production capabilities, quality control, or export licenses. They simply act as intermediaries, adding layers of cost and risk. In our sourcing experience, a buyer once discovered in 2026 that their "factory" was merely a small office in a residential building, subcontracting all production to an unvetted third party. This led to severe quality issues and delayed shipments, as the broker had no direct control over the manufacturing process.

### Matching Chinese Legal Registrations with Pro-forma Invoices

The cornerstone of verifying a Chinese supplier's legitimacy lies in meticulously matching their official Chinese legal registration name with all corresponding trade documents, especially the Pro-forma Invoice (PI). Every legally registered company in mainland China has a unique, official Chinese name (usually in simplified Chinese characters) and a Unified Social Credit Code. This information is publicly accessible on government databases.

It's critical to obtain the supplier's official business license and then cross-reference the Chinese name on it with the beneficiary name on the PI and any other official communication. Discrepancies, even minor ones, are major red flags. For a comprehensive guide on this, we recommend you learn how to [verify a China business license](/blog/step-by-step-guide-how-to-verify-a-china-business-license-online) online. This step is non-negotiable to ensure you are dealing with the actual legal entity.

## How to Verify Chinese Supplier Bank Details Before Transferring Funds

Once you've confirmed the legal entity, the next critical hurdle is to **verify Chinese supplier bank details** before initiating any payment. This is where many scams occur, often involving sophisticated invoice hacking or redirection to fraudulent accounts. A legitimate Chinese factory will almost always have a corporate bank account in mainland China, registered under their official Chinese company name.

![Section 2 illustration](/images/is-your-chinese-supplier-legitimate-how-to-match-corporate-names-bank-details-body-2.png)

### Matching the Chinese Pinyin Name with Beneficiary Names

When your supplier provides their bank details for payment, pay extremely close attention to the beneficiary name. This name should be the exact official Chinese company name, transliterated into Pinyin, or the official English name registered with the bank. Any deviation, such as a personal name, a different company name, or a generic trading company name that doesn't match the registered manufacturer, is a serious warning sign.

For example, if the company's registered name is "佛山市南海区XX电器有限公司" (Foshan Nanhai District XX Electrical Appliance Co., Ltd.), the beneficiary name should reflect this, perhaps as "FOSHAN NANHAI DISTRICT XX ELECTRICAL APPLIANCE CO., LTD." or its Pinyin equivalent. If they ask you to pay "Mr. Li Ming" or "Global Trade Solutions Ltd.", it's highly suspicious. Always request a bank statement or a letter from their bank confirming the account details, stamped with the company's official chop.

### The Danger of Offshore or Personal Accounts in Sourcing

A significant red flag is when a Chinese supplier requests payment to an offshore bank account (e.g., in Hong Kong, Singapore, or other tax havens) or, worse, a personal bank account. While some legitimate trading companies might use Hong Kong accounts for convenience, a direct factory should ideally receive payments into their mainland corporate account. Payments to personal accounts are almost always indicative of tax evasion, fraud, or an unregistered business, offering zero legal protection for the buyer.

**Pro Tip / Warning**: If a supplier insists on payment to a personal account or an account in a third-party country, immediately halt the transaction. This practice makes it incredibly difficult to trace funds or seek legal recourse in case of a dispute or scam. It's one of the primary ways buyers fall victim to scams, as detailed in our guide on how to [avoid China sourcing scams](/blog/how-to-avoid-china-sourcing-scams-10-red-flags-every-buyer-must-know).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why is my supplier's bank account in Hong Kong?

A Chinese supplier's bank account being in Hong Kong is not inherently fraudulent, but it warrants extra scrutiny. Many mainland Chinese companies, especially trading companies or those involved in international trade, establish subsidiaries or use banking services in Hong Kong for easier international transactions, tax benefits, and foreign exchange flexibility. However, if your supplier claims to be a direct factory in mainland China, and *only* offers a Hong Kong account, it's a red flag. Always verify if the Hong Kong entity is a legitimate subsidiary of the mainland company and not an unrelated third party or a shell company. Request official registration documents for both the mainland and Hong Kong entities.

### Can I pay a Chinese factory via PayPal?

While it's technically possible to pay a Chinese factory via PayPal, it's generally not recommended for large B2B transactions. PayPal is primarily designed for smaller, consumer-level payments and offers limited buyer protection for B2B disputes. Transaction fees can also be higher. For significant orders, traditional bank transfers (T/T - Telegraphic Transfer) or Letters of Credit (L/C) are the standard and most secure methods, offering better traceability and legal recourse. If a factory insists on PayPal for a large order, it might indicate they are not set up for formal international trade or are trying to avoid official banking channels.

### How to spot invoice hacking scams?

Invoice hacking scams are sophisticated and involve fraudsters intercepting email communications to alter payment details on legitimate invoices. To spot them:
1.  **Verify Any Change**: Always be suspicious of last-minute changes to bank details, especially if communicated via email.
2.  **Cross-Reference**: Compare the bank details on the invoice with previous invoices or your supplier's official company registration documents.
3.  **Out-of-Band Verification**: If you receive new bank details, *always* verify them using a different communication channel, such as a phone call to a known, trusted contact at the supplier's company. Do not reply to the email or use contact information from the suspicious email.
4.  **Look for Discrepancies**: Check for subtle changes in email addresses (e.g., `supplier@gmail.com` instead of `supplier@company.com`), grammatical errors, or unusual urgency in the request.
5.  **Secure Communication**: Use secure communication platforms for sensitive financial information whenever possible.
