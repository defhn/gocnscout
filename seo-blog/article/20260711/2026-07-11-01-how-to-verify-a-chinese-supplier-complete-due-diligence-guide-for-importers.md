Navigating the complexities of global sourcing requires meticulous due diligence, especially when engaging with suppliers in China. The integrity of your supply chain hinges on accurately verifying your partners. Before paying any Chinese supplier, verify these 5 things:

- **Company Registration**: Active status and matching scope.
- **Factory Address**: Verifiable physical industrial footprint.
- **Export History**: Real shipment records and volumes.

### Common Red Flags of Unverified Chinese Exporters

Identifying red flags early is crucial to avoid China sourcing scams. In our experience, several indicators frequently signal potential issues with unverified Chinese exporters. These often include:

- **Inconsistent Information**: Discrepancies between company names on business licenses, bank accounts, and communication channels. For instance, if a supplier's email address uses a generic domain (e.g., Gmail) rather than a corporate one, or if the company name on their website doesn't match their official registration, it's a significant warning sign.
- **Unusual Payment Terms**: Demands for 100% upfront payment, payments to personal bank accounts, or requests to send money to a third-party country. We always advise buyers to verify Chinese supplier bank details meticulously.
- **Lack of Transparency**: Reluctance to provide factory addresses, audited financial statements, or detailed production process videos. A legitimate factory should be proud to showcase its operations.
- **Too Good to Be True Pricing**: Extremely low prices compared to market averages for similar products often indicate compromised quality or a scam.
- **High-Pressure Sales Tactics**: Suppliers pushing for quick decisions without allowing sufficient time for due diligence.
- **Generic Product Photos**: Using stock images or photos clearly taken from other websites, rather than showcasing their actual production line or unique products.

Recognizing these red flags is the first step in protecting your investment and ensuring a secure supply chain. ### A Sourcing Case Review of a Ghost Factory Conflict

*(Based on anonymized supplier cases reviewed by the auditing team. Details have been changed for privacy.)*

A sourcing case we reviewed involved a buyer who lost approximately $50,000 after failing to verify the supplier's actual manufacturing capabilities. The buyer, an e-commerce retailer, was sourcing a new line of electronic gadgets for the 2026 holiday season. They found a "manufacturer" online with an impressive website, professional-looking product photos, and competitive pricing. The supplier provided a business license and even a video tour of what appeared to be a bustling factory floor.

However, the buyer skipped a crucial step: independently verifying the factory's physical address and actual operational status. After an initial 30% deposit, communication became sporadic. When the final payment was due, the supplier demanded it be sent to a different bank account, citing "internal restructuring." The buyer, under pressure to meet deadlines, complied.

The shipment was delayed, and when it finally arrived, the products were of significantly lower quality than samples, and many units were non-functional. Further investigation revealed that the "manufacturer" was a small trading company operating out of a shared office space. The factory video was generic stock footage, and the business license, while real, was for a different company name with a similar product scope, subtly altered to mislead. The original factory address provided was for an unrelated industrial park. The buyer had effectively paid a middleman who then outsourced to a low-cost, unvetted producer, resulting in a complete failure to meet quality standards and a substantial financial hit.

### Lessons Learned: Implementing Strict Vetting Protocols

This case highlights critical lessons for all importers. The primary takeaway is that a superficial review of documents is insufficient. Importers must implement strict vetting protocols that go beyond basic checks. Key lessons include:

- **Independent Verification**: Always cross-reference information provided by the supplier with independent, third-party data sources.
- **Physical Presence Confirmation**: If possible, conduct or commission a physical factory audit. If not, use tools that can confirm the registered address and operational status.
- **Bank Account Scrutiny**: Ensure the beneficiary bank account is a corporate account in China, matching the registered company name. Any deviation should be a major red flag. This is why it's vital to [verify Chinese supplier bank details](https://www.gocnscout.com/blog/is-your-chinese-supplier-legitimate-how-to-match-corporate-names-bank-details).
- **Phased Payments**: Structure payment terms to minimize upfront risk, with significant portions tied to production milestones and final quality inspection.
- **Contractual Safeguards**: Implement robust contracts that clearly define quality standards, delivery timelines, and dispute resolution mechanisms, referencing Incoterms like FOB or DDP where appropriate.

By learning from such failures, importers can fortify their sourcing strategies and build more resilient, trustworthy supply chains.

### Checking Corporate Registration Status via Official Databases

The first and most fundamental step in verifying a Chinese supplier is to confirm their corporate registration status. Every legitimate company in China is registered with the Administration for Market Regulation (AMR), formerly known the Administration for Industry and Commerce (AIC). Their official database, the National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System (NECIPS), also known as GSXT (国家企业信用信息公示系统), provides public access to basic company information.

To verify a China business license, you'll need the company's official Chinese name or its Unified Social Credit Code (USCC). This 18-digit code is unique to each entity and is the most reliable identifier. You can then search on the GSXT portal to confirm:

- **Legal Name**: Ensure it matches the name on their business license and other documents.
- **Registration Status**: Active, revoked, or dissolved.
- **Registered Capital**: An indicator of the company's size and financial backing.
- **Business Scope**: What products or services the company is legally permitted to provide.
- **Legal Representative**: The official person responsible for the company.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I verify a Chinese company for free?

Yes, you can perform basic verification of a Chinese company for free using official government websites. The National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System (GSXT) (gsxt.gov.cn) allows you to search for a company's registration details, legal status, and business scope using its official Chinese name or Unified Social Credit Code. However, these free resources often provide limited information and may require some familiarity with the Chinese language. For in-depth due diligence, such as export history, legal disputes, or comprehensive background checks, commercial platforms like GoCNScout offer more detailed, actionable data.

### What is the official registry database in China?

The official registry database for companies in China is the National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System (国家企业信用信息公示系统), commonly known as GSXT. This platform is managed by the State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) and provides public access to registered company information, including business licenses, legal representatives, registered capital, and any administrative penalties. It is the primary source for verifying the legal existence and basic operational status of any company registered in mainland China.

### How do I know if a supplier is a real factory?

Determining if a supplier is a real factory versus a trading company requires a multi-pronged approach. First, check their business license: a factory's business scope will typically include "manufacturing" or "production." Second, request a factory audit or a live video tour of their production lines. Look for signs of actual manufacturing equipment, raw materials, and finished goods. Third, analyze their export data through platforms like GoCNScout; real factories often have consistent, high-volume export records directly from their factory location. Finally, ask for specific certifications (e.g., ISO 9001, BSCI) and verify their authenticity with the issuing bodies. A trading company may claim to be a factory but will struggle to provide concrete evidence of manufacturing operations.
