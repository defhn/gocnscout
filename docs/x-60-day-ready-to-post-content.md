## Day 4 — Six research signals

### Post A — Thread

**1/7**

Before I contact a Chinese supplier, I want at least six research signals.

None proves the company is right for the order. Together, they help me ask better questions.

**2/7 — Product fit**

Does the company appear focused on the product I need, or does its catalog cover ten unrelated categories?

Broad catalogs are not automatically bad, but they change what I need to verify.

**3/7 — Location**

Is the company located near a relevant manufacturing cluster?

Location can suggest access to materials, components and skilled labor. It does not prove production ownership.

**4/7 — Business scope and company type**

What is the company registered to do?

I compare the public business description with the products being offered and note inconsistencies for follow-up.

**5/7 — Website and product evidence**

Does the website show a coherent product range, consistent company identity and useful production information?

A website is evidence to inspect—not proof by itself.

**6/7 — Exhibition or customs records**

These records can help establish identity and history.

They do not replace current operational checks.

**7/7 — Independent verification**

Before money moves, I still want samples, document verification, a clear contract, bank-account matching and—when the risk justifies it—an audit or inspection.

Save this as a starting checklist.

### Post B — Short

I don’t score suppliers from one field.

Registered capital, location, website, exhibition history and product range are all incomplete signals.

The useful question is whether several independent signals tell a consistent story.

## Day 5 — A trading company is not always the wrong choice

### Post A — Short

I don’t automatically reject a supplier because it may be a trading company.

For a large, specialized order, factory-direct can make sense.

For lower MOQs, mixed products or coordination across several factories, a capable trading company may be the better partner.

The problem is not the business model. It is pretending to be something else.

### Post B — Short

A broad catalog can be a clue that a company trades rather than manufactures.

But I would never make that judgment from the catalog alone.

I’d compare the business scope, address, product evidence, quotation detail and willingness to support an audit.

## Day 11 — Factory or trader framework

### Post A — Thread

**1/6**

“Factory or trading company?” sounds like a simple question.

In practice, I treat it as a hypothesis to test—not a label I can infer from one database field.

**2/6**

I start with product concentration.

Does the company focus on a coherent family of products, materials and processes?

An unrelated catalog raises questions, but it does not settle the answer.

**3/6**

Then I compare its registered business scope and address with what it claims to manufacture.

An industrial address is useful context. It is not proof of ownership.

**4/6**

I look for process evidence:

- Equipment relevant to the product
- Quality-control steps
- Engineering detail
- Consistent factory imagery
- Audit availability

Stock photos do not count.

**5/6**

During quotation, I watch how the company handles technical questions.

Can it explain materials, tolerances, tooling, failure modes and lead-time assumptions?

Specific answers are more useful than a factory badge.

**6/6**

Finally, I verify through samples, documents and—when justified—an independent audit.

The goal is not to avoid every trader. It is to understand who will actually control production and quality.

### Post B — Short

I’m cautious when a supplier insists it owns a factory but avoids every question about equipment, process control and audit access.

The label matters less than operational transparency.

## Day 15 — From 100 names to 10 candidates

## Day 16 — Payment-detail changes

### Post A — Short

If a supplier changes its bank details just before payment, I stop the transfer.

I verify the request through a second communication channel, confirm the beneficiary matches the contracting entity and investigate why the change occurred.

One delayed payment is cheaper than sending money to the wrong account.

### Post B — Short

Email is not enough for verifying a last-minute bank-account change.

The supplier’s mailbox may be compromised even when the message looks normal.

I use a previously verified phone or video contact and document the confirmation.

## Day 19 — Seven questions before samples

### Post A — Thread

**1/7**

Before I pay for a sample, I want answers to seven questions.

A good sample is useful. A good sample from the wrong process is not.

**2/7**

1. Is the sample made by the same facility and process intended for mass production?

2. Are the material and components identical to the proposed production specification?

**3/7**

3. Which parts are stock and which are customized?

4. What will change between sample and production—tooling, finish, packaging or tolerances?

**4/7**

5. Who pays for revisions if the sample misses the agreed specification?

I want this understood before the first sample arrives.

**5/7**

6. Which tests or documents are required for the target market?

I do not assume an existing report covers my exact model, material or date range.

**6/7**

7. What is the approval record?

Photos are useful, but a signed specification, approved sample and revision history are stronger controls.

**7/7**

The sample stage is not just about appearance.

I use it to test how the supplier communicates, documents changes and controls the path to production.

### Post B — Short

A perfect hand-made sample can create false confidence if mass production uses a different process.

I always ask what will be identical—and what will change—when the order scales.

### Post C — Engagement

What is the most expensive sample mistake you have seen?

Wrong material, wrong dimensions, wrong finish—or a sample that could not be reproduced in production?

## Day 31 — Using public supplier data responsibly

### Post A — Long-form post

I use public supplier data to reduce uncertainty—not to manufacture certainty.

Before I contact a company, structured records can help me understand its product category, location, public history and available identifiers. That is valuable because it turns a blank search box into a researchable longlist.

But every record has a boundary.

An exhibitor record tells me that an entity appeared in an exhibition source. A customs registration number helps me identify an exporter record. A website shows what a company chooses to publish. None of those facts proves current capacity, factory ownership, product quality or compliance with my target market.

My workflow is therefore layered:

1. Use databases to discover and organize candidates.
2. Cross-check identity and product fit across independent sources.
3. Ask technical questions that expose process knowledge.
4. Verify documents against the exact legal entity and product.
5. Approve samples against a written specification.
6. Use audits or inspections when the order risk justifies them.
7. Match contracts and bank beneficiaries before payment.

Good supplier data saves research time. Good procurement controls protect the transaction.

I’m building GoCNScout and BamwoodChina around that distinction.

### Post B — Short

I don’t want a database to make the buying decision for me.

I want it to show me where to investigate, what evidence exists and which questions remain unanswered.

That is a more modest promise—and a more useful product.
